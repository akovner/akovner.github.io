In my [last post](/sampled-democracy/2018/07/23/sovereign-truth.html), I proposed a new way for a society to decide it's most fundamental questions: the jurga. This requires, however, that those questions be formulated in such a way that a jurga render a decision. In addition, there must be an event which triggers the formation of a jurga to consider a given question, and a way to create the alternatives that will be presented to the jurga. I will address those issues in this post.

I take it as self evident that how jurgas are formed and what questions they are given to consider is a fundamentally political question, with all of the baggage that politics carries. Therefore, these questions must be resolved by entities that are chosen directly by the population at large. The public directly chooses entities which then formulate policy, which is then presented to a jurga for a final decision. There are two choices for what these enties might be: individual candidates or political parties. The traditional view has been that voting for people is the most meaningful and accountable way to select the political class. The U.S. constitution does not even mention parties; the delegates to the Constitutional Convention viewed them with suspicion. I wish to challenge this view. Parties, properly managed, are the highest expression of our political ideals. Their policy positions are generally more stable and transparent than that of individuals. They must bear the consequences of policy over a much longer period of time. A U.S. president is limited to two terms, making 8 years the practical window of accountability. Not so for parties. Parties are less susceptible to cult of personality. Finally, and most importantly, parties becomes tribes that appeal to voters' core sense of themselves. They are simply a better and more natural way to organize the political class.

The problems with parties comes from the fact that they are not an institutionalized part of the government. Since they are technically informal associations outside of the official government, they cannot be effectively regulated or funded, leaving them highly susceptible to capture by extremist or corrupt interests. It also results in a bait-and-switch at the election booth: Voters *think* they are voting for an individual, but what they are getting is overwhelmingly just a replacement level representative of the party. This is especially true for members of the legislature who are not among the party leadership.

In my system, voters vote *only* for parties. They do so using [ranked voting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranked_voting) to maximize voting expressiveness while minimizing vote tallying pathologies. There is another great advantage of voting this way: voting can be continuous. The fact that voting occurs in a single event every couple of years (in the U.S.) is deeply problematic. It makes our elections subject to manipulations both internal and external, and leads to grandstanding and obstructionism of the worst kind. Continuous elections have the potential to reduce these evils considerably. Voters would be divided into, say, 52 distinct cohorts, and each cohort would vote during one week out of the year. Any voting method could be used - vote by mail, vote at a kiosk at the DMV, etc.

And what form would this voting take? It would be a ranked list of political parties on a variety of aspects of governance. I divide governance into the following areas:

1. executive
    1. domestic policy **(xd)**
    1. foreign & military policy (**xf**)
    1. finance & economic policy (xe)
1. legislative
    1. legislative priorities (lp)
    1. legislative solutions (ls)
1. judicial (j)

Each of these six areas would correspond to a ranked list that each voter would populate with political parties in order of preference. This means there is a tremendous need to present voters with a reasonable number of political parties, all of which are competent to fulfill each of the functions listed. Heuristically, there should be at least 3 parties, and no more than 6. Three at least are needed to prevent the problems associated with the U.S. political system, which is a zero-sum slugfest. But having more than six (or so) creates fragmentation. I would limit the number of parties by constitutional fiat.
